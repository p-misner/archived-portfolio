Challenge

Citizens sometimes wonder how much their votes count. After all, one recent presidency was decided not by ballots, but by five judges — largely because nobody could discern some voters’ intentions. And so, this project involves exploring ways to redesign voting ballots to remove any uncertainty about the voter’s choice.

Background

According to the Brennan Center's Voting Rights and Election Project, "An election is not held to tests a voter's ability to follow instructions, but to receive instructions from the voter's as to which candidates they will elect. No legitimate public purpose is served by designs that distort those instructions." In the 2000 presidential election, bad ballot design in Palm Beach County, Florida arguably cost Al Gore the election . Using overly ornamental arrows and a confusing layout, the ballot were hard to decipher and far from intuitive. Rather then recording the voter's preferred candidate these ballots tested their ability to follow instructions

With the rise of more complex voting systems like ranked-choice voting designing understandable ballots is becoming necessary to preserving the integrity of our democratic system. Following the Brennan Center's recomendations for better ballots, I set to work designing a touch screen voting app for ranked choice voting.
